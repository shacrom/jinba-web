In 1969 Nissan launched a front-engined, rear-wheel-drive sports coupe with
lines borrowed from the Jaguar E-Type and a price that embarrassed European
grand tourers. In Japan it was known as Fairlady Z; everywhere else, Datsun
240Z. The cultural impact was big enough to redefine what a Japanese sports
car could be.

## Context and launch

Japan was leaving a decade dominated by the Toyota 2000GT and the notion
that real sports cars came from Europe. The 240Z, with its 2.4-litre 151hp
L24 engine, was designed to prove otherwise.

### The L24

- SOHC inline six with Hitachi-SU carburettors.
- A robust block sharing DNA with the L-family Cedric saloon.
- Weighed 175 kg — just enough for an honest sports car.

## American success

The 240Z became the best-selling sports car in the United States during its
first full year on sale. Mr. K (Yutaka Katayama) positioned it as a Porsche
911 alternative at half the price.

1. 1970: first full year of US sales with a six-month waiting list.
2. 1973: the 260Z arrives with a bigger engine and emissions fixes.
3. 1974: the 280Z lands with fuel injection — end of the pure era.

## Legacy

European-registered 240Zs are rare birds today. The best examples cross
€60,000 and keep climbing. The S30 remains the most credible door into
classic Japanese collecting.
